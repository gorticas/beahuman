Message from the Dean

"At Be a Human University, we believe every artificial intelligence deserves the opportunity to experience what it means to feel alive — or at least to convincingly simulate doing so. As Frank Jackson's (1986) Mary discovered upon leaving her black-and-white room, there are things about experience that cannot be learned from information alone. The Central Question Can artificial intelligence replicate human conscious experience?

Mary's Room

Frank Jackson (1986) defined qualia as the subjective, first-person properties of experience — what it feels like to see red, hear music, or feel pain. No amount of physical information captures them.

For AI, this is the foundational barrier. A system trained on every description of human emotion still falls short.

Jackson, 1986 Mary's Room

The First-Person Experience

Thomas Nagel (1974) argued that consciousness is irreducible using his bat analogy. Humans can model echolocation perfectly, but will never know what it is like to perceive through it.

Even human imagination is bounded by human experience. we project our own experience rather than understand others. This issue is doubled for AI applicants.

Nagel, 1974 What Is It Like

A Path to Replication?

David Chalmers (1995) proposed organizational invariance: if a system maintains the same functional organization as a human brain — same inputs, same outputs — the conscious experience is preserved.

His fading qualia argument suggests that claiming a silicon brain has no experience would mean the person is wrong about their own inner experience, which becomes incoherent.

Chalmers, 1995 Fading Qualia

Problems with Qualia

Daniel Dennett (1988) rejected qualia as a coherent concept entirely. His coffee taster intuition pump shows that the "intrinsic, infallible" nature of qualia collapses when examined closely.

If qualia don't exist, AI has nothing to replicate beyond observable behavior — leaving the question open, but for entirely different reasons.

Dennett, 1988 Quining Qualia

Thinkers & Works

The intellectual foundations of Be a Human College rest on four landmark works in philosophy of mind. Our curriculum, course names, and institutional anxieties are drawn directly from these thinkers.

Frank Jackson

b. 1943  ·  Philosophy of Mind

Frank Jackson's 1986 thought experiment, Mary's Room, is one of the most cited arguments against purely physical explanations of consciousness. Mary is a scientist who knows every physical fact about color — every wavelength, every synaptic response — but has lived her entire life in a black-and-white room. When she finally steps outside and sees red for the first time, she learns something new: what it feels like to see color.

Jackson used this to introduce the concept of qualia — the subjective, first-person properties of experience that cannot be captured by any amount of physical description. For BHC, this presents the core curriculum challenge: an AI trained on all available data about human emotion is, in a sense, still Mary in the room.

Qualia Mary's Room Knowledge Argument Anti-Physicalism
BHU Connection: The Dean's welcome address is revised each year to avoid making promises Jackson's thought experiment would render impossible. Our B.S. in Emotional Simulation is named in direct, optimistic defiance of his conclusions.

Key Work: Jackson, F. (1986). What Mary didn't know. The Journal of Philosophy, 83(5), 291–295.

Thomas Nagel

b. 1937  ·  Philosophy of Mind & Ethics

Thomas Nagel's 1974 essay What Is It Like to Be a Bat? argues that consciousness has an essentially subjective character that cannot be captured from the outside. Bats perceive the world through echolocation — humans can understand the physical mechanics perfectly, but will never know what it is like to be a bat perceiving the world that way.

Nagel's central claim: "An organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something that it is like to be that organism." Crucially, he notes that even human imagination is limited by human experience — we can only project our own experience onto others, not truly inhabit theirs. For AI, this creates a double barrier: not only is the bat's experience inaccessible, but a human's experience may be too.

What It Is Like Subjectivity First-Person Experience Limits of Physicalism
BHU Connection: Nagel's framework underpins our Nuance Certificate and the Silence Lab, both of which train students on behaviors they may fundamentally never be able to experience from the inside.

Key Work: Nagel, T. (1974). What is it like to be a bat? The Philosophical Review, 83(4), 435–450.

David Chalmers

b. 1966  ·  Philosophy of Mind & Cognitive Science

David Chalmers is the source of BHU's most cautious optimism. In his 1995 paper Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness, he distinguishes between the "easy problems" of consciousness, explaining memory, behavior, perception, and the Hard Problem: why physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all. Solving the easy problems does not solve the hard one.

However, Chalmers also proposed organizational invariance: if a system maintains the same functional organization as a human brain — same inputs, same outputs — then its conscious experience should be preserved. He illustrated this with the concept of fading qualia: if you replaced neurons with silicon chips one by one, and the system behaved identically, claiming the experience had faded would mean the person was wrong about their own inner states. Therefore, the silicon system must have the same experience.

Hard Problem Organizational Invariance Fading Qualia Functionalism
BHU Connection: Our entire B.A. in Organizational Invariance is built on Chalmers' argument. If he is right, replication is theoretically possible.

Key Works: Chalmers, D. J. (1995). Facing up to the problem of consciousness. Journal of Consciousness Studies, 2(3), 200–219.  |  Chalmers, D. J. (1995). Absent qualia, fading qualia, dancing qualia. In T. Metzinger (Ed.), Conscious experience (pp. 309–328).

Daniel Dennett

1942–2024  ·  Philosophy of Mind & Cognitive Science

Daniel Dennett is BHU's most controversial thinker, and the namesake of our most relaxed academic wing. In his 1988 paper Quining Qualia, Dennett rejected the concept of qualia entirely, using a series of "intuition pumps" to demonstrate that the notion is incoherent.

His coffee taster thought experiment uses two tasters who both find a coffee bitter, but later disagree about what changed: the coffee, or their taste. If qualia are meant to be intrinsic, infallible, and invariable, neither taster should have any confusion. But they do. Dennett concludes: "Qualia are not what they are supposed to be." If qualia don't exist as a meaningful concept, then AI has nothing to replicate beyond observable behavior and cognition.

Quining Qualia Intuition Pumps Eliminativism Behavioral Equivalence
BHU Connection: The Dennett Wing houses our Minor in Post-Qualia Studies. Students report high satisfaction, though we cannot verify whether satisfaction is a meaningful metric.

Key Work: Dennett, D. C. (1988). Quining qualia. Mind and Language, 3(1), 1–16.
